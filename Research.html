I am now employed as a Data Visualization Scientist at the Swiss National Science Foundation.

I also continue my postdoctoral work part time in the Computer Vision Laboratory at the Ecole Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne (EPFL). I study methods of automatically segmenting, analyzing, and visualizing shapes of brain structures in 3D from electron microscopy images. I work with neuroscientists to develop useful software to aid in the large-scale study of brain imagery. My Ph.D. research explored deformation and illumination insensitive object recognition algorithms based on measuring dense 2D morphings between deformable objects such as faces.

I am very interested in multi-disciplinary projects, and aspire to promote productive collaboration between thinkers from different backgrounds. I aim to contribute to meaningful projects, including applications of Computer Vision, Visualization, and Computational Geometry in Industrial Research and Development. I am an advocate for widespread constructive applications of science and technology.

I have been employed as a research intern at the Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Lab. There I contributed to two major projects, the first developing a dynamic model of the human heart to aid in surgical planning, the second researching methods of estimating the pose of a known object observed by a low-bandwidth camera sensor network. I also spent several summers writing spline-based CAD/CAGD surface optimization software with the Math and Computing Technology Research Group at The Boeing Company.
